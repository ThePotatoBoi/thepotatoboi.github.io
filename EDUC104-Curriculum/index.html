"The curriculum and teaching are as inseparable from one another as the skeleton is from the human body"

(Walker & Soltis, 2004)

Based

A curriculum is more than a lesson plan. It has philosophical and historical bases and is formulated based on the aims of our education, on the needs of our society. It must always lead to specific learning goals and objectives along with the three domains:

Cognitive
(Knowledge)

Affective
(Values)

Psychomotor
(Skills)

Planned

And I don't mean by any simple planning, of course. In fact, Curriculum planning is entirely different from teaching the curriculum. As Mangrum and Strichart (2012) say,

"A curriculum is a comprehensive plan, designed with intent, and organized with purpose."

Dynamic

The curriculum today may not fit the scene tomorrow, next week, or a year after! Since the needs of society (and learners) could change at any moment, the curriculum must adapt (like how we adapted to online/modular modalities when the pandemic occurred).

Curriculum is
customizable.

What is a "curriculum," and why should we care? Well, there is no common ground in defining curriculum. And this web infographic will delve into varying yet intersecting definitions of curriculum out there—all in one piece based on how I understood it!

Anywhere

The curriculum is not only bound inside the school. It could apply outside school grounds—at the learner's home, not by homework but by the values they can practice (for example, learning socialization isn't only exclusive at schools, learners can practice such at their homes).

Democratic

As Walker and Soltis (2004) stated,

"Students, parents, and the public have rights in curriculum matters, too."

This concept is one of John Dewey's philosophies, telling us that democracy implies participation. This view can help students become active and responsible members of their community.

The ultimate goal of the curriculum is "to help learners develop the knowledge, skills, and values that will enable them to contribute to society and participate fully in a democratic and globalized world"

(Biesta, 2013, p. 50)
