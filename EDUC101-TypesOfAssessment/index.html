Assessment, Learning Resources, and Instructional Accommodations:
Introduction and Types of Assessment

Introduction

Image from IRIS Center.

Assessments play an important role in facilitating classroom inclusion. It helps educators distinguish not just student needs but also what accommodation to consider for the disadvantaged ones.

So, to honor this ground, ponder the question below:

Question to Ponder:

How do we address students at a disadvantage and students with a disability in a classroom setting?

Assessment

Assessment refers to the wide variety of methods or tools that educators use to evaluate, measure, and document the academic readiness, learning progress, skill acquisition, or educational needs of students.

How do we classify assessments?

There are many classifications of assessment used by educators, which include but are not limited to the following:

  • Pre-assessments or diagnostic assessments are administered before students begin a lesson, unit, course, or academic program.
  • Formative assessments are in-process evaluations of student learning that are typically administered multiple times during a unit, course, or academic program.
  • Summative assessments are used to evaluate student learning after a specific instructional period—typically at the end of a unit, course, semester, program, or school year.
  • Placement assessments are used to “place” students into a course, course level, or academic program.
  • Screening assessments determine whether students may need specialized assistance or services or are ready to begin a course, grade level, or academic program.

Assessments can be also classified by their purpose (e.g. assessment of learning, assessment for learning, and assessment as learning).

(Assessment Definition, 2015)

Learning Resources

Learning resources are any resource–print and non-print materials–and online/open-access resources which support and enhance–directly or indirectly–learning and teaching.

These may include printed or digital materials (textbooks, flashcards, computer software), tools, equipment, and aids, (chalkboard, computer, projector), and facilities (classroom, school gym, science/computer laboratory).

What about the learning resources for disabled students?

We have specialized learning formats that include but are not limited to the following:

  • Braille is a system of reading and writing through touch, made up of raised dot patterns for letters, numbers, and punctuation marks.
  • Large prints are hard copies that larger than the standard ones and sometimes there may be more white space for students who had difficulty focusing on the text.
  • Audio formats provide information through recorded or non-recorded speech. It may be synthesized electronic speech spoken by a computer or other device.
  • Digital text presents information through text, audio, images, or any combination of these. Some are modified to address students who had difficulty seeing colors.

(PACER Center & the National Center on Accessible Instructional Materials at CAST, 2011)

Instructional Accommodations

Instructional accommodations are the changes in the way a student accesses learning, without changing the actual standards a student is working toward.

In addition, Lee (2022) delved deeper and devised different types of accommodations:

  • Presentation: A change in the way information is presented.
  • Response: A change in the way people complete assignments or work.
  • Setting: A change in the learning or working environment.
  • Timing and scheduling: A change to the time for a task.

Examples of accommodation based on disability/disadvantage

Disability or Disadvantage Example Accommodation
Dyslexia
  • Presentation: devise audio-based materials
  • Response: allow audio-based inputs
Dysgraphia
  • Response: permit audio-recording instead of taking notes
Dyscalculia
  • Presentation: instead of numbers, present topics using concrete terms or examples
  • Timing: chunk out the problems in an interval
Near-sightedness
  • Setting: let them sit in the front row of the class
Poor concentration
  • Timing: allow frequent breaks
  • Setting: try to change the student’s seat to the least distractive location
Inability to hold pencil
  • Response: allow oral-based outputs

Observation checklists

Observation checklists are a set of questions or descriptions that assists an observer in evaluating an individual's performance and behavior. Additionally, it can be used for diagnosis purposes so that educators can devise the best accommodation to consider if there is a need.

Anecdotal Records

Anecdotal records are narrative records (or can be short stories/comments) that educators use to document a significant incident that they have observed. These can help the teacher to understand their pupils realistically.

[gallery]

Portfolio Assessments

Portfolio assessments are collections of student works associated with standards the teacher required them to learn. A portfolio assessment describes the student’s learning progression throughout the year; that is, you can regard it as a reflective storybook of the student’s development.
